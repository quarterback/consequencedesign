Consequence Design

A field guide to systemic effects, hidden logics, and institutional erosion in mediated life.

What is Consequence Design?

Consequence Design is a way of tracing how systems behave once deployed. It maps the unintended, invisible, and downstream effects of infrastructure, tools, and institutions—especially when friction is removed or context is lost. It isn’t just a critique of technology. It’s a lens for understanding how simulation-native systems shape trust, participation, and the erosion of public meaning.

Origins

This work began as an attempt to name the patterns behind interface collapse and institutional degradation. What started as interaction mapping evolved into a broader theory of how legitimacy is manufactured through dashboards, defaults, and systems that appear neutral while quietly reorganizing power.

Consequence Design emerged as a response to acceleration without accountability. It now serves as both a framing device and a design ethic.

Core Ideas

  • Second-Order Effects: Most systems are designed with narrow goals. The deeper impacts—the ones that ripple, accumulate, or distort—often go unmeasured and unaddressed.
  • Administrative Aesthetics: Legitimacy is increasingly projected through dashboards, performance metrics, and “neutral” interfaces. Governance becomes simulation.
  • Parasitarchy: Institutions can be hollowed out and repurposed, retaining the form of function while becoming tools of extraction or illusion.
  • Friction as Signal: Friction isn’t failure—it’s information. Smoothness often masks harm, while obstacles can reveal where systems truly operate.
